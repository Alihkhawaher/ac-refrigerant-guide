It sounds like you are referring to **R134a**, which is the standard refrigerant used in automotive air conditioning systems for vehicles manufactured roughly between 1994 and 2020. 

Charging a car's AC system using a pressure gauge (often called a manifold gauge set or a DIY recharge kit) is a straightforward process, but it requires careful monitoring to avoid overcharging the system, which can damage the compressor.

Here is a step-by-step technical guide to safely charging your car's AC system with R134a.

### **Tools Needed**
*   **R134a Refrigerant Can:** Preferably one that comes with a built-in pressure gauge and a trigger valve (e.g., A/C Pro, InterDynamics).
*   **Safety Glasses & Gloves:** Refrigerant is extremely cold and can cause frostbite or eye damage if it sprays out.
*   **Thermometer (Optional):** To measure the actual temperature of the air coming out of the vents.

---

### **Step-by-Step Procedure**

#### **1. Locate the Low-Pressure Service Port**
Open the hood and locate the two AC service ports. 
*   **Low-Pressure Port:** This is on the larger diameter aluminum tube. It is usually capped in **blue or black** and marked with an **"L"**. 
*   **High-Pressure Port:** This is on the smaller diameter tube, usually capped in **red or black**, and marked with an **"H"**.
*   *CRITICAL WARNING:* **Only connect your gauge to the Low-Pressure port.** The fittings are designed with different diameters so you cannot mix them up. If you connect a DIY kit to the high-pressure side while the system is running, the high pressure will force refrigerant back into the can, causing it to explode.

#### **2. Connect the Gauge and Refrigerant**
*   Screw the charging hose onto the top of the R134a can.
*   Connect the quick-disconnect fitting on the other end of the hose to the **Low-Pressure port** on the car. It should slide on smoothly. If it doesn't, check that you aren't trying to force it onto the high-pressure port.
*   *Do not puncture the can yet.*

#### **3. Start the Vehicle and AC**
*   Start the engine and let it idle.
*   Turn the AC to the **Maximum Cold** setting.
*   Set the blower fan to the **Highest** speed.
*   Turn on the **Recirculation** mode (this ensures the system is cooling the interior air rather than pulling in hot outside air).
*   Open the car doors or windows for ventilation.

#### **4. Puncture the Can and Monitor Pressure**
*   Look at the gauge. If the system is completely empty, the pressure might be near 0 psi, and the AC compressor clutch (on the front of the compressor pulley) might not be engaging. 
*   Squeeze the trigger on your kit to puncture the seal on the can. 
*   **Add refrigerant in short bursts** (about 5 to 10 seconds at a time). Release the trigger between bursts to let the pressure stabilize.
*   *Note on Can Orientation:* Read the instructions on your specific can. Most recommend keeping the can **upright** so you are adding refrigerant *gas* rather than *liquid*. Adding liquid refrigerant directly into the low side can "slug" and destroy the compressor.

#### **5. Check the Pressure Chart**
*   Look at the ambient temperature chart printed on the gauge or the refrigerant can. 
*   The chart correlates the temperature under the hood (ambient temperature) with the correct low-side pressure. 
*   **General Rule of Thumb:** 
    *   At 70°F–80°F (21°C–26°C), the low-side pressure should be roughly **25–35 psi**.
    *   At 90°F+ (32°C+), the low-side pressure should be roughly **40–45 psi**.
*   Keep adding short bursts of refrigerant until the gauge needle enters the "green" or optimal range for the current outside temperature.

#### **6. Check Vent Temperatures**
*   While monitoring the pressure, place a thermometer in the center dashboard vent. 
*   A properly charged system should blow air between **35°F and 45°F (2°C to 7°C)** at the center vents.
*   Listen to the AC compressor. It should stay engaged continuously. If it rapidly clicks on and off every few seconds (short-cycling), the system is either still low on refrigerant, overcharged, or has a failing pressure switch.

#### **7. Disconnect and Finish**
*   Once the pressure is in the correct range and the air is blowing cold, **stop adding refrigerant**. 
*   *WARNING:* **Do not overcharge the system.** An overcharged system will have excessively high pressures, poor cooling performance, and will likely destroy the AC compressor. It is always better to be slightly undercharged than overcharged.
*   Close the valve on your kit (if it has one) to trap the remaining refrigerant in the can for future use.
*   Quickly pull the quick-disconnect collar back and pull the hose off the Low-Pressure port. A tiny hiss of air is normal, but the port has a valve inside that will immediately seal the system.
*   Screw the plastic dust cap back onto the Low-Pressure port.

### **Troubleshooting Tips**
*   **Compressor won't turn on:** If the pressure is below ~20 psi, the low-pressure safety switch prevents the compressor from turning on to prevent it from running without oil. Adding a small amount of refrigerant should raise the pressure enough to trigger the switch and engage the compressor.
*   **System is leaking:** If the system was empty or you have to add refrigerant every few weeks, you have a leak. R134a kits often include a UV dye or mechanical sealant. If you use a sealant, be aware that some professional repair shops will refuse to work on a system that has chemical sealants in it because it can clog their recovery machines.
*   **Environmental Warning:** Venting R134a into the atmosphere is illegal in many jurisdictions (including the US under EPA regulations) and is harmful to the environment. Only add what the system needs, and store the partially used can properly.
